Overview

       One of the biggest problems I had during the development of Bluelog was the simple act of testing new versions. I had collected a small army of Bluetooth devices via yard sales, eBay, and Craigslist, but even with them all on I couldn't break more than 20 simultaneously discoverable devices. This forced me to take a lot of trips to local malls during peak shopping hours, but this got pretty annoying very quickly. Driving 20 minutes each way and hoping for the best (I never knew if I would get 20 or 200 devices logged) made this impractical for serious development.

Around the time I started working heavily on Bluelog 0.9.9, I was made aware of the concept of Bluetooth traffic monitoring, wherein Bluetooth scanners are deployed on the side of busy streets to estimate the number and speed of vehicles passing by. Reading about this technology made me realize that I was going about this whole thing the wrong way; why chase around Bluetooth devices when I could simply wait for them to drive by?

Operation Street Sweep was my attempt at capitalizing on this concept by deploying a long-term Bluetooth monitoring station in an urban environment to collect large amounts of real-world data. The project ran from May 5th, 2011 to May 10th, 2011 in Eastern Pennsylvania, generating data which proved invaluable during the development of Bluelog.

Hardware Setup

       The hardware setup for this project consisted of a TP-LINK MR-3220 running OpenWRT Backfire and the AIRcable Host XR coupled with a 9 dBi omnidirectional antenna. The MR-3220 was chosen simply because it was the cheapest OpenWRT-compatible router that featured a USB port. Any OpenWRT device with a USB port and BlueZ installed should be compatible with Bluelog, and I have received reports of it running successfully on the Buffalo WZR-HP-G300NH and the Ubiquiti RouterStation Pro.

The equipment was placed in the windowsill, within range of a busy intersection and shopping plaza. This proved to be an ideal location, as it was able to scan cars sitting at the intersection and anyone who stopped to enter one of the stores. If there was any downside to this location, it was that so many of the targets were passing through the intersection as part of a daily commute. This means that the same targets would pop up every 8 hours or so as they went back and forth between their homes and jobs. This is an interesting avenue of research in itself, but unfortunately was not the goal of this project.

Lessons Learned

       During the course of this project, a number of unexpected complications arose which made things slightly more difficult than I anticipated. Of course, these issues are themselves important pieces of data and will be taken into account during future large-scale scanning projects.

Operation Street Sweep was made possible by, in large part, the porting of Bluelog to OpenWRT. The immediate advantages of use OpenWRT was clear: low energy consumption, compact size, silent operation, etc. One thing I didn't take into account was the increased development effort required in terms of processor and memory optimizations. For example, running Bluelog on a device with only 32 MB of RAM made memory leaks much more obvious. A memory leak which affected earlier versions of Bluelog was only made apparent when Bluelog was found to be consuming nearly 50% of the device's free RAM after running for 12 hours.

After I got Bluelog running reliably for longer than 12 hours, a new issue came to light which never occurred during my previous tests. It turned out that, after logging 200 - 300 devices, Bluelog would start to re-log them as if they were new discoveries. I spent a few days tracking down the bug which was causing this issue, only to discover a fairly obvious mistake on my part in a piece of code which I thought was done 4 months ago.

With the two software issues out of the way, and Bluelog finally ready to embark on a long-term mission, it was the hardware setup's turn to give me a problem. As it turns out, the Host XR with a 9 dBi antenna is able to jam WiFi communications at close range. This simple fact made it occasionally difficult to communicate with the MR-3220 during active scanning. At first I was unsure about how to approach the problem, as unplugging the Host XR would obviously cause the scan to fail and crash Bluelog. In the end, I had to remove the antenna from the Host XR each time I wanted to download the log files from the MR-3220. This kept the scan running, but minimized the Host XR's jamming ability. In the future, a more directional antenna pointed at the target zone could be used to minimize the adverse effects of the AIRcable's output energy, and should additionally improve range and scanning accuracy. Naturally, I could also use Ethernet to connect to the MR-3220 rather than WiFi, if the situation allows it.

This project brought to light issues which I might never have discovered otherwise, proving just as useful as the data that it would eventually return. The limitations imposed by the embedded hardware and the rigors of running unattended for 120+ hours necessitated code optimizations and fixes which benefit all Bluelog users, regardless of platform. The problems stemming from the AIRcable's effect on local wireless communications also proved an interesting example of how Bluetooth could be used in a non-conventional DoS against other forms of communication.

Resulting Data

       While the problems that were discovered and fixed during Operation Street Sweep were extremely beneficial to the further development of Bluelog, the goal of this project was always to collect real-world data about discoverable Bluetooth devices in a typical urban environment. One of the biggest misconceptions about Bluetooth security is that there aren't that many active Bluetooth devices in the wild, and I hope that projects like Operation Street Sweep can help set the record straight.

During the 5 day mission, Bluelog was able to successfully log 1,776 unique devices. In addition, a further 820 were logged with partial data (due most likely to fast moving vehicles). There are still improvements I can make to the scanning code, but I think the results are fairly impressive and show that an exploitable ecosystem of Bluetooth devices is out there waiting.
